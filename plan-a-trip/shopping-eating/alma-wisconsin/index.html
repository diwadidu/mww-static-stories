A 90-year-old man wearing a red cardigan and ``old-age combat hat'' sat in a wheelchair 500 feet above Alma, looking down at the river winding past the Wisconsin town.

``Beautiful, isn't it,'' he commented. ``We just saw a couple of eagles fly by, bald ones. And see those islands over there? I love them.''

In the distance was Lock and Dam 4 and the wide river plain, broken by the channels and long clumps of green that mark the beginning of the Upper Mississippi River National Wildlife and Fish Refuge, stretching all the way to Rock Island, Ill. But Ray Studer hadn't come all the way from St. Cloud to give any fish refuge.

``We were up here two years ago and saw that F-shaped fishing dock over there,'' said his daughter, Rosanne Healey. ``Dad's been talking about it ever since.''

Alma has a handicapped-accessible municipal dock, off Beach Harbor Road between Rieck's Park and downtown. But Studer planned to fish from the Great Alma Fish Float across the river, which fetches people and lets them fish all day on the float, which has a cafe and bait and tackle shop.

The bird's-eye view from Buena Vista Park, two miles up by road, is the pride of Alma, pronounced EL-ma by locals. The town is attractive, too, though it only has two streets: storefronts along Highway 35 and houses backed into the bluff behind it. All of them are on the National Register of Historic Places.

Alma was founded by Swiss immigrants in 1848, and until recent, tourists could sample rouladen, geschnetzeltes and roesti in its restaurants.

The 1868 building that now houses the Fire & Ice coffee and ice-cream shop once was a winery operated by the Huber brothers of Switzerland. In the 1880s, they shipped in 45 barrels of California grapes a week by rail.

Now, its owners sell ice cream, rent rooms and cultivate a back patio carved into the bluff, its rocky terraces planted with hosta, phlox and ferns and its floor with white deck chairs and a pillowed bench.

It's a kind of remnant of the town he knew in his teens.

``Alma used to have very narrow streets with lots of trees,'' he said. ``It used to be that everywhere there were nooks and crannies in rock walls, things you discovered.'' Then the highway was widened and the town's look changed, though not its character.

``It still has that rough edge of a river town,'' he said. ``People come to fish, hunt and drink. They're not going to chi-chi it up here too much.''

Trip Tips: Sightseeing around Alma

Getting there: Alma is across the Mississippi River from Kellogg, Minn., between Wabasha and Winona.

2016 events: Aug. 12-13, Swiss Heritage Days. Sept. Music & Arts Festival.

Buena Vista Park:

Castlerock Museum: It's open Fridays and Saturdays 1-4 p.m. Admission is $5, $3 for students.

Great Alma Fishing Float: It's open 6 a.m. to 6 p.m. from late March through October. Cost of $15 includes boat ride, minnow buckets and landing nets, and there's a cafe that serves breakfast and lunch and a bait and tackle shop.

For pickup, prop up the white sign on the small dock just south of the municipal dock with the red light, behind Buffalo Trading Co. 651-380-7296 or 651-380-5322.

Boating and swimming: There's a municipal beach with a bathhouse north of downtown off Beach Harbor Road, next to the Alma Marina.

Accommodations: The closer an inn is to the river, the closer it is to train tracks. If you think the trains will bother you, bring ear plugs.

See Great River Retreat and Guest House on Facebook.

On Main Street, the newly renovated Blue Door Inn has four suites and two apartments with two beds, two baths and patio and/or porch.

The Tritsch House B&B is at the foot of the bluffs, buffered from train noise. The 1902 Victorian has four rooms, three with two-person hot tubs.

The Hotel de Ville is right above the train tracks, overlooking the dam, and includes a one-bedroom and a two-bedroom suite, both with a full kitchen. 612-423-3653.

Camping: There are first-come, first-served spaces in Rieck's Lake Park north of town, $15 including showers.

Dining:

Pier 4 Cafe and Smokehouse, which overlooks the lock and dam, is known for its barbecue and breakfasts. It's closed Tuesdays.

The Stone Barn in the bluffs between Alma and Nelson serves pizza made in a wood-fired brick oven, as well as wine, beer and ice cream. There's also an antiques story in the granary. It's open for dinner Fridays-Sundays from May to early October.

In the bluffs above Cochrane, Suncrest Garden Farms serves wood-fired pizza Fridays in May and September and Thursdays and Fridays from June through August.

Fire & Ice serves espresso drinks, ice cream and pastries.

Bird-watching: The Wings Over Alma observation deck and nature/visitor center is at 118 N. Main St.

Information: Alma tourism, 608-685-3330.
