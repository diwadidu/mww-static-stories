Pottery in Cambridge, Wisconsin

When artisans and shopkeepers followed potters into Cambridge, Wis., the former resort village became a magnet for shoppers.

When Christmas approaches, people unpack those cute little ceramic villages, arranging the tiny shops and steepled churches on mantels or in windows with strands of white twinkle lights.

But if you don�t have your own miniature village, you can always visit the real thing.

Cambridge, Wis., is not made of ceramics, exactly, but it does owe a lot to pottery.

In 1975, when the country-d�cor fad was gathering steam, Jim and Tina Rowe bought a blacksmith shop in this village of 1,000, in the countryside east of Madison.

They opened an antiques shop and pottery works, saw the prices at which old pieces of salt-glazed pottery were selling and began to make their own crocks and plates with the distinctive blue-and-gray finish, achieved by throwing salt into the flames during firing.

They were a big hit. Today, Rowe Pottery Works is the nation�s largest producer of salt-glazed pottery, and Cambridge is known as the pottery center of the Midwest.

Along with other potters came artisans and shopkeepers, who moved into the brick storefronts of Main Street, which slopes past the village square to Koshkonong Creek and West Side Park.

In winter, ice skaters circle on the park�s rink, and skiers head out along the wooded trail that follows the creek.

During Country Christmas weekend in December, carolers and Dickensian street urchins roam the streets, where visitors ride on horse-drawn wagons. On the porch of the pottery works, Jim Rowe roasts chestnuts.

When I arrived this summer, after a day of bicycling on the nearby Glacial Drumlin State Trail, I checked in first at the Cambridge House. I had some of the wine, cheese and crackers Dotti had put out, then strolled down Main Street, looking in the windows.

All year round, day-trippers and weekenders come here, mainly to shop.

Then I walked down to the creek and the park, where so many fireflies were flitting around it looked as if the town had already put up its twinkle lights.

Back at the Cambridge House, Bill Krieger told me about the town�s past, when tourists from Illinois came to spend the summer at spring-fed Lake Ripley, also the spot where local boy Ole Evinrude first tested his outboard motor.

Trip Tips: Cambridge, Wisconsin
