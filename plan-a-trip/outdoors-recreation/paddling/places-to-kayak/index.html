10 great kayaking rivers

Easy does it on these mostly rock-free streams.

IMG:RIGHT

Whitewater kayaking is a lot of fun to watch, but not everyone wants to go bumping through froth on an obstacle course of rocks.

Many of the best-known whitewater-kayaking rivers � the Kettle in eastern Minnesota, rivers of Minnesota's North Shore, the Presque Isle on Michigan's Upper Peninsula � are not suitable for beginners.

Beginning paddlers, especially timid ones, should look for a river that�s mostly flat, has reliable flow most of the summer and is served by an outfitter who can offer advice and support.

On most rivers, you'll want a shorter boat of 10 to 14 feet. On the bigger rivers � the St. Croix, the Namekagon and the Wisconsin � you can use a sea kayak, though it won't turn as easily as shorter boats.

If you don't have a boat yet, outdoors stores hold free demos, starting next week. Try out kayaks there and then rent one for a weekend, to make sure it's for you.

Kayaking for all kinds of boats

Cannon River from Cannon Falls, Minn. The Cannon River from Cannon Falls to Welch, an hour south of the Twin Cities, has a few gentle rapids that quality as Class I.

ADS:4

Welch Mill Canoeing & Tubing rents kayaks and provides shuttles, 800-657-6760. The river is very popular, so reserve in advance, and paddle first thing in the morning if you want to avoid crowds and see wildlife.

Red Cedar from Menomonie, Wis. This western Wisconsin river starts with a stretch of mild whitewater, then heads south toward the Dunnville Wildlife Area past sheer walls of golden sand.

Paddlers can stash bicycles at the takeout and shuttle themselves back on the Red Cedar State Trail or get a shuttle from Roscoe's Red Cedar Outfitters, 715-235-3866.

St. Croix River between Minnesota and Wisconsin. Wild River Outfittersin Grantsburg, Wis., rents kayaks and provides a shuttle on the 13 miles between Nelson�s Landing and the Wisconsin 70 bridge near Grantsburg, which has Class I rapids. 715-463-2254.

For plastic boats only

When rivers are studded with rocks, or water is low, plastic boats are more forgiving of mistakes. Here are some fun rivers to paddle, if you have a plastic boat.

Bois Brule in northwest Wisconsin. The 12-mile section northeast of Solon Springs, between Stones Bridge and Winneboujou Canoe Landing, is easiest and most historic; the next four miles to Brule have Class II rapids.

Call Brule River Canoe Rental, 715-372-4983, 392-3132. For details, see Paddling the Bois Brule.

Kinnickinnic from River Falls, Wis. The eight-mile stretch of the Kinni between River Falls and Kinnickinnick State Park in western Wisconsin is lively but rarely dangerous, and because the river is spring-fed, it�s particular refreshing in summer.

In River Falls, Kinni Creek Outfitters provides kayaks and shuttles, 877-504-9705 or 715-425-7378. For details, see A spin down the Kinni.

More good rivers

Many other outfitters rent kayaks, as do outdoors stores if you're willing to tote them yourself. Wisconsin�s Activity Guide lists 76 places to rent boats in 57 towns; get one at 800-432-8747. Travel Wisconsin also listsoutfitters and destinations.

In Minnesota, the DNR sends out free canoe-river guides that include maps and descriptions of public access points and navigational features, 888-646-6367. Thirty designated water trails are described on its web site, which also reports current river levels and lists 42 places to rent boats on 15 rivers.
