Paddling the Upper Iowa

On best rivers, there's something new around every bend.

((refer)) Good canoeing routes, Page xx.

Beth Gauper, Pioneer Press

On the Upper Iowa River, canoeists paddle in the shadow of 280-foot limestone palisades.

((hed)) On best canoeing rivers, there�s something nifty around every bend

As a tourist destination, Iowa rewards independent thinkers.

Most people persist in thinking it�s one big, flat field of corn. But those who break free from the herd are in for a pleasant surprise.

ADS:4

My friend Torsten had never been to Iowa before last August, nor had he wanted to go. Then he went canoeing with me on the Upper Iowa River near Decorah.

Paddling along, we came upon 280-foot limestone cliffs, then freestanding pillars of stone called chimneys.

�This is like canoeing down a little canyon in Utah, except the rock is white instead of red,�� he noted.

Downriver, we passed the Bluffton Fir Stand, a remnant of the balsam firs and Pacific yews common here 5,000 years ago, when the climate was colder and wetter.

�Wow, this reminds me of what you�d see hiking in the Sierras,�� he said.

Poor Iowa � to compliment it, people often say it looks like some place else. But the scenery along this northeast Iowa river is distinctive enough that National Geographic Adventure magazine declared canoeing there to be one of America�s Best Adventure 100, along with rafting in the Grand Canyon.

It�s no secret to Iowans, of course, who joined us in droves on a hot weekend in late August. The water was low, and those extra Twinkies came back to haunt some people, who had to get out repeatedly and pull themselves and their canoes over sandbars.

Campers were hanging out along the banks, trying to stay cool. On one sandy patch a gaggle of little girls played, and one of them started to dash across until she saw us coming.

�I guess we need a Kid Crossing sign,�� her father said.

This being Iowa, we also spotted a herd of cows in the river, though all but one headstrong heifer retreated by the time we reached them. We watched swooping turkey vultures and hawks; one couple we met reported seeing bald eagles the day before. Swallows had affixed dun-colored condos to the soft rock bluffs.

But it was the dramatic scenery that drew our attention. The rock spires really did look like Utah, and the cliffs got even more spectacular around Bluffton, rising vertically from the river, which twists and turns around them. Photographs of these palisades are published so often that this section of the river is referred to locally as �calendar corner.�� Downriver from Bluffton, the bluffs became more wooded, rising 330 feet from the river.

Loath to get off the water, we stopped paddling as soon as we saw our take-out bridge in the distance. But the river propelled us forward, and soon the van from Chimney Rock Campground picked us up.

I�d love to go back to the Upper Iowa this time of year, when the water is flowing faster.

Just across the Minnesota border, not far from the Root River system, the Upper Iowa River winds toward Decorah. Its most picturesque and popular stretches are the 15{ river miles from Kendallville to Bluffton and the 16 miles between Bluffton and Decorah. Because of low water levels, we paddled some of each when we stayed at Chimney Rock Campground, which ferried us upriver to bridge 4 and took us out downriver at bridge 7, after about three hours of paddling.
