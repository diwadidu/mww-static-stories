Shoreline water taxi cruise from Sister Bay

Shoreline water taxi cruise from Sister Bay

We got more views of mansions on Shoreline Charter's water-taxi cruises, piloted by captain Geoff Cronk. This time, we headed north from Sister Bay, toward the Ellison Bay headlands.

There, we had a view of Ellison Bay Manor, known locally as "the Big House,'' a 35,000-square-foot mansion that was finished in 1996 and is believed to be the largest single-family house in Wisconsin.

It has six kitchens, eight fireplaces, 10 bathrooms, a two-story library, an indoor pool and spa with waterfalls and a 5,000-square-foot master suite with a 1,000-square-foot closet, said tour guide Mike Brodd.

It was for sale at $5.9 million, quite a deal considering it sold for $18 million in 2005.

Nearby, we saw a steel-framed glass box jutting over the 200-foot cliffs. It was built by Ben Heineman, head of the Chicago and Northwestern Railway and a member of President Lyndon Johnson's kitchen cabinet. His claim that it was an office, worthy of a tax deduction, was upheld by the Supreme Court, Cronk said.

Down the bluffs, we saw a house that has 171 windows to take in the view of the bay beyond the native northern white cedar trees. Homeowners aren't supposed to trim the cedars unless they have a grandfathered-in "view easement,'' Cronk said. It's easy to tell the homes that do � their trees are shaped like lollipops.

Along the shoreline, the famously tenacious cedars cling to the tops of gleaming white blocks of limestone. They're also called arbor vitae, tree of life, because tea made from their needles has enough vitamin C to ward off scurvy in sailors.

As we went by Ellison Bluff, built of rock that once was sand at the bottom of the sea, Brodd passed around a honeycomb fossil. Then he pointed out the unbroken treeline of Door Bluff Headlands County Park, which preserves 155 acres and a stunning view of the bay.

"It's the oldest forest in Wisconsin, never cut, with arbor vitae that's 400 to 450 years old,'' he said. "It's the one place where you can see exactly what 17th-century explorers saw from a canoe.''
