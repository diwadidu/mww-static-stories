Baby steps in the snow

At Trees for Tomorrow, beginning skiers get the push they need.

Sometimes, a person who wants to look slick has to look stupid first.

Standing in the snow one morning in February, I and 10 other skiers walked like gorillas, bouncing on our thighs. We rolled our hips like hula queens and stuck out our arms as if we were practicing tai chi on the beach. We used lobster grips and pretended to cross a creek, hopping from boulder to boulder.

It was all at the bidding of Sandy Lotto, who learned Alpine skiing before she was 3 and was an expert at that until, at 22, she tried cross-country skiing as an intern naturalist at Trees for Tomorrow in Eagle River, Wis. Now, Lotto skis in every spare moment, racing and also teaching around Wisconsin's north woods.

``I lucked out; it changed my life,'' she says, with a modest laugh.

One February, she taught a women's ski clinic at Trees for Tomorrow, an environmental-learning center with its own oval ski track. After morning exercises, we got into the striding tracks and went back and forth, our pole straps tightened over our knuckles, bending forward from the waist, swinging our arms as we tried to stretch out our glides. It turns out the gorilla practice was useful, after all.

``If you learn only one thing this weekend, it should be to keep your arms low and relaxed,'' Lotto said. ``If you do that, you'll be worlds ahead of everyone else.''

When we watched the videos later, one person stood out.

``Whoa, who's that?'' said Pam Runge of Milwaukee. ``Look at that; it looks like a demo video. All that jogging paid off.''

It was my friend Becky, the only person in the group using bamboo poles, three-pin boots and ancient skis that hadn't had a glide-wax base in years. Yes, she's a runner � but also a good example that equipment doesn't make the skier.

Phobias about waxing and proper ski gear have driven many people to snowshoeing, Lotto says. But, while sophisticated techniques and equipment do make skiing easier, she says, they're not crucial for beginners.

``The big thing is to get outdoors and find a spot you enjoy,'' she says. ``Then, start out shuffling, and as you begin feeling more comfortable, you can start lengthening your glide. That's where it gets fun.''

But skating � the faster, more graceful but more demanding technique used by racers � is a different matter entirely. Becky and I had tried it, with dismal results.

``I tried that once and almost died,'' Becky said. ``I didn't see how people could do that for very long.''

Along with the other women, we were ready to try it again. First, we skied without poles, which made our ankles ache. Then we skied with poles, but became hopelessly confused by the different styles used for climbing, sprinting or flat land � V-1, in which both poles and one ski are planted at the same time; V-2, with double-poling on each stride; and V-2 alternate, with poling between glides.

It was a lot like ballroom dancing. Just as we caught a rhythm, we'd run into the back of someone's skis and lose it again. And we couldn't fathom how anyone could bound up a hill skating � we had to work harder just to get up the indiscernible uphill grade on Trees for Tomorrow's oval.

``It's hard work, honestly,'' Lotto said. ``The beauty of cross-country skiing is there's always something to work on.''

But when we saw ourselves on video, we were amazed � none of us looked as clumsy as we felt.

There are many ways to learn the basics; in St. Paul, Como Ski Center offers lessons in classical and skating ski techniques on Saturday mornings and Monday, Tuesday and Wednesday evenings.

This year, Trees for Tomorrow isn't offering a ski clinic. But Sandy Lotto is, at ABR in Ironwood on Michigan's Upper Peninsula; Nicolet College in Rhinelander; and her own Adventurer's Hostel in Eagle River, which nearly always can be counted on for good snow.

The fun way to practice skiing, of course, is to take a weekend and go off into the woods, to a resort that offers instruction as well as rentals. Here are a few of the best:

Bear Paw Inn: This outdoor-adventure resort in northeast Wisconsin is a good place for all winter sports � snowshoeing and mushing, as well as skiing on 15 kilometers of trails. Guided skis will be held Jan. 1, 14 and 28 and Feb. 25, $25 including skis. Lodging only ranges from $22 for a rustic cabin to $104 for a cabin with fireplace and kitchen. Weekend programs include meals. (715) 882-3502, www.bearpawinn.com

Maplelag: This friendly resort in northwest Minnesota has fostered skiers of all ages, on 53 kilometers of superb trails at all levels of difficulty. The lodge burned a year ago, but a new one is under construction, and the resort plans to reopen this winter. The weekend rate, for two nights and six meals, is $179-$205 adults; $124 children 8-14 and $98 children 4-7, with a 10 percent discount for families; the rate for two midweek nights is $152 adults and $91 and $72 children. Call (800) 654-7711, www.maplelag.com

Palmquist's: This resort in north-central Wisconsin is a good place for beginners and children: Its 35 kilometers of trails are gentle, with bypasses cut around the manmade hills. There's also an ice-skating pond and sledding hill, snowshoeing and sleigh rides behind two Belgian draft mares. Lodging is in cabins and the farmhouse, where everyone gathers for meals. Call (800) 519-2558, www.palmquistfarm.com

Telemark: This northwest Wisconsin resort has had its ups and downs, but survives because of its 40 kilometers of beautiful trails, many quite difficult. There's a pool, ice-skating pond, tubing hill and sleigh rides. Ask about packages; a la carte, lodgings start at $75 for a lodge room and go to $300 for suites and condos with Jacuzzis. (877) 798-4718, www.telemark-resort.com.

Giants Ridge: This Alpine resort on Minnesota's Iron Range has 70 kilometers of cross-country trails, with some snow-making capability. Lodgings vary from the luxury Lodge at Giants Ridge and Villas at Giants Ridge to inexpensive nearby hotels; ask about packages. (800) 688-7669, www.giantsridge.com

Gunflint Trail: In far northeastern Minnesota, lodges share 200 kilometers of groomed trails, some lit, and snow that's the most reliable in Minnesota. On the Central Gunflint Trail, Bearskin Lodge has very nice traditional cabins and lodge suites. There's a large Hot Tub Hus, and a lodge in which delicious four-course dinners are served. (800) 338-4170, www.bearskin.com. Golden Eagle has a mixture of older and new cabins, some with whirlpools, and a 1945 lodge. A naturalist leads excursions and activities. (800) 346-2203, www.golden-eagle.com. In the Upper Gunflint, Gunflint Lodge is a full-service resort with modern townhouse units that have VCRs, fireplaces and whirlpools or saunas. (800) 328-3325, (800) 362-5251, www.gunflint.com

Trip Tips: Learning to ski

Como Ski Center: Starting Dec. 9, snow permitting, St. Paul Parks and Rec will give 1{1/2}-hour lessons at 10 a.m. Saturdays and 6:30 p.m. Mondays-Wednesdays, $13. Rental of classical skis is $7. For a brochure, call (651) 266-6445.

ABR, Active Backwoods Retreat: This private, 35-kilometer trail system just across the Wisconsin border in Ironwood has become known for its reliable snow; day passes are $8. Sandy Lotto will give an intermediate-level clinic in skating and striding there Dec. 2, $30. Owners Dave and Eric Anderson also give 45-minute lessons, $10 individual, $25 for a group of up to four. Call (906) 932-3502, www.michiweb.com/abrski. The center rents a rustic cabin, $30, and Ironwood has many inexpensive hotels; call (906) 932-1122, www.ironwoodmi.org

Adventurer's Hostel: Sandy Lotto is giving Women in the Winter Woods ski clinics Dec. 29-31 out of her home, eight miles from Eagle River. Cost is $40. Room at the hostel is $15 per person, with private bath and use of a kitchen. Call (715) 479-8773. Another nice place to stay is the Pinewood B&B, with eight attractive rooms, $92-$135, and spacious common areas in a part of the lodge built in 1934. There's also a three-bedroom home next door, $250. (715) 477-2377, www.inn-at-pinewood.com

Lotto also is holding a Women's Day on Snow ski clinic Dec. 16 out of Nicolet College in Rhinelander. Cost is $40, including lunch. Call (800) 585-9304.
