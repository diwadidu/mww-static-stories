What to do and see in Mantorville, Minnesota

In a southeastern Minnesota village, nostalgia is the currency of the present.

Back in Minnesota�s early days, the town of Mantorville was a contender.

In the 1850s, it was a stagecoach stop on the route from the Mississippi to the western plains. It sat in a green little valley on a branch of the Zumbro River, and the local limestone was in demand all over southern Minnesota.

But the railroad and main highways bypassed Mantorville, and it was eclipsed by such neighboring towns as Rochester, where the brothers Mayo were founding a clinic.

Yet history has a way of turning back on itself. Today, people from those bigger towns are happy to forsake their split-levels and office buildings for a little trip back in time.

They wander Mantorville�s 12 blocks of limestone and clapboard buildings, all of them on the National Register, and dine under milk-glass sconces at the atmospheric 1854 Hubbell House.

They take in one of the melodramas performed at the 1918 Opera House and browse through the antiques shops that flank it.

Civic leaders gilded the lily in the 1970s by installing wooden boardwalks all over town. The planks made Mantorville look like a movie set and were inscribed with the names of the people who had ``bought�� them for $1. Visitors loved it, especially when they spied the name of a celebrity. But three years ago, the rustic but rough-hewn planks had to be ripped up.

``It was kind of a shame; people wandered all over town reading those boards,�� says Arlene Draeger, office manager of the Hubbell House. ``But there were too many lawsuits. I know the Hubbell House itself had to buy one lady a pair of new shoes after she broke off her heel on them.��

Mantorville / Melodrama on the Zumbro River

Highbrow it's not. But the theater housed in the 1918 Opera House of Mantorville, Minn., is as endearing as an old quilt.

Since 1973, the Mantorville Theater Companyhas been having a swell time putting on melodramas, some of them original, in this tiny village on the Zumbro River, 15 minutes west of Rochester.

Audiences are encouraged to boo the villains, who are always dastardly, and cheer the heroes, who are always true and strong.

The town, once a stagecoach stop on the route from the Mississippi to the western plains, is on the National Register of Historic Places, including the 1857 Hubbell House, a restaurant visited over the years by such celebrities as Gen. U.S. Grant and Mickey Mantle and still a popular dining destination, 507-635-2331.

Tourists also like to browse in the antiques and gifts stores and at the chocolate shop.

It's 1� hours south of the Twin Cities.
